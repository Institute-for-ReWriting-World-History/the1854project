In April of 1854, a multitudes of scholars published a book on Outlines of the Philosophy of Universal History, Applied to Language and Religion in London. It contained a brief chapter on The Arian settlers and Aboriginal races of India that—despite relying on tenuous evidence, would go on to form the basis for the Aryan Invasion Theory. No aspect of Indian history that would be written here has been untouched by the years of academic dogma that followed. On the 169th anniversary of this fateful moment, it is finally time to tell this arc of Indian history truthfully.

The 1854 Project

The 1854 Project is an ongoing initiative from The Institute for ReWriting World History that began in April 2023, the 169th anniversary of the publication of Outlines of the Philosophy of Universal History, Applied to Language and Religion. It aims to challenge academic dogma and present world history from an Indic perspective.

Image from The Evolution of Man (1879), Prose from The 1619 Project (2019)

In May of 1498, a fleet of ships appeared on the horizon, near Calicut, a trading port on the malabar coast of India. It carried four cloaks of scarlet cloth, six hats, four branches of corals, twelve almasares, a box with seven brass vessels, a chest of sugar, two barrels of oil and a cask of honey, which were presented as gifts to the Zamorin to open trade relations. No aspect of the republic that would be formed here has been untouched by the years of subjagation that followed. On the 525th anniversary of this fateful moment, it is finally time to tell our story truthfully.

The colonial project to (re)interpret an unknowable past of the Indian subcontinent began in 1776 with the publication of the English translation of Manusmṛiti—to preempt, in the words of the officiant, the pundits from dealing out “Hindoo law as they please, and make it at reasonable rates, when they cannot find it ready made.”

The Code of Capital (2019) by Katharina Pistor

The British Raj, a continuation in part of the Mughal Raj, operated on three axes: knowledge, power, and being, each taking place in a transformation of the World Economy driven by the Transatlantic Slave Trade.

Empire of Cotton: A Global History (2015) by Sven Beckert

Colonial scholarship was (and arguably is) “a continuation of political commerce, a carrying out of the same by other means,” if you want to understand the modes of colonialism, you have to start with coloniality of knowledge. 

Literacy and power in the ancient world (1994) by Alan K. Bowman, Greg Woolf

The railways were built using a guarantee system with the aim of advancing British military and strategic interests on the subcontinent and acted to hasten India’s dependency (and deindustrialization—”from a manufacturing country into a country exporting raw produce“), constituting one part in a larger scheme to carry out a drain of wealth.

The Looting Machine (2015) by Tom Burgis

The différance in acculturation of the loanword caste, from the Iberian casta, on the Indian subcontinent, when contrast with the near absence in the Americas, illustrates the degree to which British attempts to catalog Indic civilization as “petrified in the merely natural classification” succeeded in substituting caste (and its peripheral meanings) as nomen for heterogeneous social structures on the Indian subcontinent.

Orientalism (1978) by Edward W. Said

The reality of the British Raj was one of man made want, famine and mortality, and was written about (and hence known) as far back as the publication of An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations—the marginal benefits were the result of Indians adapting to the accident.

Anti-Oedipus: Capitalism and Schizophrenia (1972) by Gilles Deleuze, Felix Guattari 

The historiography of the Long Peace (1945–) is rooted in the whig interpretation of history and obscures man is in the midst of the ultimate revolution, the challenge for scholars seeking to unravel this knot is finding the metes and bounds of history outside the limitations imposed by coloniality.

The Shadow of the Great Game (2005) by Narendra Singh Sarila

The Great Game, “a condition of Warre of every one against every one,” has made the job of know thyself difficult for every one—the celebrants, for whom such “traumas are no more than occasions for it [the ego] to gain pleasure,” and discontents, who find themselves underwriting the exhuberance, can rewriting world history from an Indic perspective serve as a panacea?
